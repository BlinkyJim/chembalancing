Quick Summary

Rule #1: Only change coefficients (big numbers in front), never subscripts.

Rule #2: Balance metals → non-metals → polyatomics → H → O (last).

Rule #3: Diatomic elements: H₂ N₂ O₂ F₂ Cl₂ Br₂ I₂ — always paired.

The Balancing Method (5 Steps)

Every time you balance an equation, use this exact order. Do not deviate.

STEP 1

Write the equation with all correct chemical formulas for reactants and products.

STEP 2

Balance atoms that are NOT O, H, or polyatomic first. Focus on elements that appear in one compound on each side first.

STEP 3

Balance polyatomic ions next — if a polyatomic ion appears unchanged on both sides, treat it as a single unit.

STEP 4

Balance hydrogen (H) next.

STEP 5

Balance oxygen (O) last. Then do a full atom count check on both sides.

Worked Example

Unbalanced:

C₃H₈ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Step 1 — Balance Carbon: There are 3 C on the left → put 3 in front of CO₂

C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + H₂O

Step 2 — Balance Hydrogen: There are 8 H on left → need 4×H₂O

C₃H₈ + O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Step 3 — Balance Oxygen (last): Right side: 3×2 + 4×1 = 10 O → need 5×O₂

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O ✓

Check: C=3✓ H=8✓ O=10✓

The Golden Rules

  • ONLY add or change coefficients (numbers in front of formulas)
  • NEVER change subscripts — that changes the substance entirely
  • Use lowest whole-number coefficients in final answer
  • If coefficient is 1, you don't write it (1H₂O = just H₂O)
  • Always do a final count of every atom on both sides
  • If stuck, try doubling your current coefficients

Common Traps on Tests

❌ Changing H₂O to H₃O to "balance" hydrogen — this is illegal. H₃O is a completely different substance.

❌ Forgetting to multiply ALL atoms in a formula. 2Fe₂O₃ means 4 Fe and 6 O, not 2 of each.

❌ Balancing oxygen before hydrogen in a hydrocarbon combustion reaction.

❌ Forgetting that O₂ and H₂ are diatomic — writing them as single atoms.

✅ TIP: If you get stuck and can't balance, check that you have the right chemical formulas first — the formulas might have an error.

✅ TIP: For double displacement, start balancing with the most complicated-looking group (usually the polyatomic).

Diatomic Elements

These 7 elements always exist as pairs of two atoms when they appear alone in nature. You MUST write them with a subscript 2.

H₂
N₂
O₂
F₂
Cl₂
Br₂
I₂

Memory trick: "Have No Fear Of Ice Cold Beverages" → H, N, F, O, I, Cl, Br

If a question says "hydrogen gas" on its own, you write H₂, not H. Same with oxygen gas → O₂.

Why Does This Matter?

When balancing: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O You start with 2 O atoms on the left (because O₂). If you forgot the subscript and wrote "O" you'd count 1 and balance everything wrong.

State Notation (Physical States)

Written in brackets after a chemical formula. They describe the physical state of each substance in the reaction.

(s)Solid — fixed shape, doesn't flow
(l)Liquid — flows, not in water
(g)Gas — spread out, no fixed shape
(aq)Aqueous — dissolved in water

State notation does NOT affect how you balance. It's purely informational about the physical state of each substance.

Example: 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g)

Law of Conservation of Mass

Proposed by Antoine Lavoisier in 1789. It states:

"Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction."

This is the entire reason we balance equations. If you start with 4 hydrogen atoms, you must end with exactly 4 hydrogen atoms. Nothing disappears and nothing new appears — it only rearranges.

This is also called the Law of Conservation of Matter. Your teacher may call it either name on the test.

What This Means on a Test

  • Atoms on left side = atoms on right side (always)
  • Total mass of reactants = total mass of products
  • Balancing equations IS conservation of mass in action
  • Subscripts show bonding (fixed) — coefficients show amounts (variable)

The 5 Reaction Types — Quick Reference

Synthesis

A + B → AB

Two or more reactants join to form ONE product

Decomposition

AB → A + B

ONE reactant breaks into two or more products

Single Displacement

A + BC → AC + B

One element replaces another in a compound

Double Displacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Two compounds swap their ions/atoms with each other

Combustion

Fuel + O₂ → oxides + energy

Rapid reaction with oxygen, releasing energy

Synthesis Reactions

Two or more simple reactants combine to form ONE more complex product.

General: A + B → AB

Examples:

2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s) 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l) CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq)

If you see exactly ONE product: it's synthesis.

Note: Elemental combustion is a specialized form of synthesis — a metal + O₂ → metal oxide. The difference is combustion releases energy rapidly.

Decomposition Reactions

ONE complex reactant breaks down into two or more simpler products. The opposite of synthesis.

General: AB → A + B

Examples:

2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g) 2HgO(s) → 2Hg(l) + O₂(g)

If you see exactly ONE reactant: it's decomposition.

Decomposition reactions often require energy input (heat, electricity, or light) to break the compound apart.

Single Displacement Reactions

One element replaces a similar element inside a compound.

General: A + BC → AC + B

This happens in two situations:

SITUATION 1

Metal displaces a metal — a more reactive metal kicks out a less reactive one.

Li(s) + NaCl(aq) → LiCl(aq) + Na(s)
SITUATION 2

Non-metal displaces a non-metal — a more reactive non-metal kicks out a less reactive one.

F₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → I₂(aq) + 2KF(aq)

⚠️ Exception: A metal can also displace hydrogen from an acid, because hydrogen forms a positive ion (H⁺) just like metals do.

Zn + 2HCl → H₂ + ZnCl₂

Double Displacement Reactions

Two ionic compounds swap their positive ions (cations) with each other to form two new compounds. Think of it as a double swap.

General: AB + CD → AD + CB

The cations and anions (positive and negative ions) trade partners.

Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI₂(s) + 2KNO₃(aq)

When balancing double displacement: start with the most complicated looking group first — usually the polyatomic ion.

Key sign: Two compounds on the reactant side, two compounds on the product side. Often produces a precipitate (solid) shown with (s).

Combustion Reactions

A fuel reacts rapidly with oxygen (O₂), releasing a large amount of energy. The products are oxides (usually CO₂ and H₂O for hydrocarbons).

Complete Combustion (Hydrocarbons)

Enough oxygen is present to fully react all the fuel.

CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy

Example:

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O + energy

Balance order: Carbon → Hydrogen → Oxygen (always last for combustion)

Incomplete Combustion

Not enough oxygen — produces extra harmful products.

CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + CO + C + energy

⚠️ CO (carbon monoxide) is produced — colourless, odourless, and deadly. This is why running engines in enclosed spaces is dangerous.

Elemental Combustion

Metals and some non-metals (carbon, phosphorus) also combust.

General: Element + O₂ → Oxide + energy 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ + energy 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(g) + energy

Elemental combustion is a specialized synthesis reaction — but it's classified as combustion because of the oxygen + rapid energy release.

Polyatomic Ions

A polyatomic ion is a group of atoms that act as a single charged unit. Common ones you'll see:

SULFATE

SO₄²⁻

NITRATE

NO₃⁻

PHOSPHATE

PO₄³⁻

HYDROXIDE

OH⁻

CARBONATE

CO₃²⁻

AMMONIUM

NH₄⁺

The Polyatomic Trick

If a polyatomic ion appears unchanged on both sides of the equation, treat it as a single unit when counting and balancing.

Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂ + 2KNO₃

The NO₃ group stays intact on both sides. Count it as 1 unit, not as individual N and O atoms.

⚠️ If a polyatomic ion is BROKEN UP in the reaction (like in combustion), you cannot treat it as a unit — you count individual atoms.

Watch for brackets: Ca(OH)₂ means there are 2 OH groups → 2 O atoms and 2 H atoms.
