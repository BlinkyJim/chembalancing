The 5-Step Balancing Method

Use this exact order every single time. Do not deviate.

STEP 1

Write the correct chemical formulas for all reactants and products.

STEP 2

Balance atoms that are NOT O, H, or polyatomic first. Focus on metals, then non-metals.

STEP 3

Balance polyatomic ions — if one appears unchanged on both sides, treat it as a single unit.

STEP 4

Balance hydrogen (H) next.

STEP 5

Balance oxygen (O) last. Then do a complete atom count on both sides to verify.

Worked Example — Double Displacement

Unbalanced:

Pb(NO₃)₂ + KI → PbI₂ + KNO₃

Step 1 — NO₃ is a polyatomic ion unchanged on both sides → treat as one unit.

Left side has 2 NO₃ groups. Right side has 1. Fix K and NO₃:

Pb(NO₃)₂ + ? KI → PbI₂ + ? KNO₃

Pb: 1 on each side ✓. I: 2 on right → need 2 KI. NO₃: 2 on left → need 2 KNO₃. K: 2 on each side ✓.

Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂ + 2KNO₃ ✓

Check: Pb=1✓ N=2✓ O=6✓ K=2✓ I=2✓

The Golden Rules

  • ONLY change coefficients (big numbers in front) — never subscripts
  • Coefficients must be lowest whole numbers in the final answer
  • If coefficient is 1, you do not write it out
  • Always verify your answer by counting every atom type
  • If stuck, try doubling all coefficients on one side
  • Use LCM when you need to match an odd number of atoms

Common Exam Traps

❌ Writing H₃O instead of adding a coefficient to H₂O. Changing subscripts changes the substance.

❌ Forgetting to multiply ALL atoms. 2Fe₂O₃ = 4 Fe and 6 O — not 2 of each.

❌ Treating a polyatomic that breaks apart as a single unit (only works if it stays identical on both sides).

❌ Forgetting brackets: Ca(OH)₂ = 2 OH groups = 2 O atoms and 2 H atoms.

✅ Start double displacement with the most complex group (usually the polyatomic ion).

✅ After every adjustment, recount ALL atoms — not just the one you fixed.

The 4 Reaction Types

Synthesis

A + B → AB

Two or more reactants form ONE product.

Decomposition

AB → A + B

ONE reactant breaks into two or more products.

Single Displacement

A + BC → AC + B

One element replaces a similar element in a compound.

Double Displacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Two ionic compounds swap their ions with each other.

Quick ID Guide

SYNTHESIS CLUE

Exactly ONE product in the equation → Synthesis.

DECOMPOSITION CLUE

Exactly ONE reactant → Decomposition.

SINGLE DISPLACEMENT CLUE

One element alone reacts with a compound, and one element comes out alone as a product.

DOUBLE DISPLACEMENT CLUE

Two compounds react and produce two new compounds. Ions swap. Often produces a precipitate (s).

⚗️ Synthesis Reactions

Two or more simpler reactants combine to form ONE more complex product. Think of it as building.

General: A + B → AB

Synthesis Examples

2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s) 2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s) N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) → 2NH₃(g) 4Al(s) + 3O₂(g) → 2Al₂O₃(s) CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq)

If you see exactly ONE product → it is synthesis. No exceptions.

Balancing Synthesis Tip

Balance metals first. Then non-metals. For binary compounds (e.g. NaCl, MgO), the formula tells you the ratio of ions — use that to set coefficients.

Example: Al₂O₃ means 2 Al and 3 O per formula unit. To get whole-number coefficients for O₂ (gives 2 O per molecule), find the LCM of 3 and 2 = 6 → need 2×Al₂O₃ (6 O) and 3×O₂ (6 O), which means 4 Al.

💥 Decomposition Reactions

The exact opposite of synthesis. ONE complex reactant breaks down into two or more simpler products.

General: AB → A + B

If you see exactly ONE reactant → it is decomposition. No exceptions.

Decomposition Examples

2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) 2HgO(s) → 2Hg(l) + O₂(g) 2KClO₃(s) → 2KCl(s) + 3O₂(g) CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g) 2NH₃(g) → N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) H₂CO₃(aq) → H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Decomposition Energy Note

Decomposition reactions almost always require an input of energy (heat, electricity, or light) to break the compound apart. This is often shown with Δ (delta) above the arrow, meaning heat is applied. You don't need to balance the energy — just the atoms.

🔀 Single Displacement Reactions

One element replaces a similar element inside a compound. A more reactive element kicks out a less reactive one.

General: A + BC → AC + B

Two Situations

SITUATION 1 — Metal displaces Metal

A more reactive metal replaces a less reactive metal in a compound.

Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)
SITUATION 2 — Non-metal displaces Non-metal

A more reactive halogen replaces a less reactive halogen.

Cl₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → I₂(aq) + 2KCl(aq)
EXCEPTION — Metal displaces Hydrogen from Acid

Hydrogen in acids forms H⁺ ions just like metals, so a reactive metal can displace it.

Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)

Single Displacement Examples

Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s) Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g) Cu(s) + 2AgNO₃(aq) → Cu(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2Ag(s) Br₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → I₂(aq) + 2KBr(aq) 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g)

🔄 Double Displacement Reactions

Two ionic compounds react and exchange their cations (positive ions), producing two new compounds. Think of it as a swap.

General: AB + CD → AD + CB

The positive ion from compound 1 swaps partner with the positive ion from compound 2.

Double Displacement Examples

AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq) BaCl₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2NaCl(aq) CuSO₄(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) → Cu(OH)₂(s) + Na₂SO₄(aq) HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) FeCl₃(aq) + 3NaOH(aq) → Fe(OH)₃(s) + 3NaCl(aq)

Often produces a precipitate (s) — an insoluble solid that forms in solution. This is a key sign of double displacement.

Balancing Double Displacement

Start with the polyatomic ion — if it stays intact on both sides, treat it as one unit. Balance it first, then adjust the simpler ions around it.

For acid-base neutralization (HX + MOH → salt + H₂O): count the H and OH groups needed to cancel each other out.

Law of Conservation of Mass

Proposed by Antoine Lavoisier. States: Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.

This is why we balance equations — every atom on the left must also appear on the right. The total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.

This is the scientific law behind everything. If the teacher asks "what law requires balanced equations?" → Law of Conservation of Mass.

Polyatomic Ions — The Big Rule

A polyatomic ion is a group of atoms that acts as a single charged unit. If it appears unchanged on BOTH sides of the equation, treat it as a single unit when counting.

⚠️ If the polyatomic ion gets broken up (which doesn't happen in the 4 reaction types we study), you must count individual atoms.

Common Polyatomic Ions

Nitrate

NO₃⁻

Sulfate

SO₄²⁻

Carbonate

CO₃²⁻

OH⁻

Phosphate

PO₄³⁻

NH₄⁺
